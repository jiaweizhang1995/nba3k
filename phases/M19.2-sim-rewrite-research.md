# M19.2 — Simulation Engine Rewrite: Research

**Researched:** 2026-04-26
**Domain:** NBA team-strength → expected-wins modelling
**Confidence:** HIGH on diagnosis & algorithm choice, MEDIUM on the exact tuning constants (those need empirical fit against the 2024-25 ground truth).

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Five bullets, what's the recommended path:

1. **The current `derive_profile` is structurally broken in three ways at once.** (a) It computes `ortg`/`drtg` as *linear sums of independent per-player attribute scores* — but team defense in particular is non-additive (the literature is unanimous on this; see § 3 / § 5). (b) It uses *raw OVR*-driven `top-8` rotations with no position constraint, so PHO and CHI stack 4 PGs on the floor and the per-attribute averages still look fine. (c) It uses a single `score_sigma` for per-game variance applied to a near-symmetric ORtg/DRtg gap, which **compresses the season spread** — strong teams don't pull away enough.
2. **The fix is not "tune the weights harder."** The gap between OKC (real +12.7 NetRtg) and PHO (real -1.8) is ~14 NetRtg points. Our current model gives them similar `off_score` and only ~4 points of `def_score` gap, which after the 0.78 multiplier is ~3 NetRtg points of separation. We are off by **roughly 4×** on the defensive signal. No sane reweight of a linear sum gets you there because the inputs themselves don't carry the information.
3. **Recommended path is a two-layer model.** Layer 1: build a **team-quality vector** from rotation-aware aggregates (eFG-style shooting composite, defensive rim protection × perimeter containment as a *product* not a sum, plus a top-3-by-impact "star concentration" term). Layer 2: convert that to expected per-game point differential, then **simulate via Pythagorean expectation** (`exp = 14`) to convert season point-differentials into wins. This is the FiveThirtyEight + Basketball-Reference approach in miniature and matches the literature on what actually predicts NBA wins.
4. **Rotation construction needs to be position-aware, not OVR-aware.** Build the rotation as `1 PG + 1 SG + 1 SF + 1 PF + 1 C` starters (with secondary-position fallback), then 3 bench by *gap* rather than OVR. This single change probably accounts for 30-40% of the standings drift, because right now the engine literally can't model "OKC has Chet + Hart who never play together" — it just plays them both 70%.
5. **Variance budget needs widening, not tightening.** Real NBA `season_winpct_stddev` across teams is ~0.20 (16-17 wins), the sim is ~0.10 (8 wins). We don't need *less* per-game noise — we need a *bigger expected mean gap* so the per-game noise washes out over 82 games. The `score_sigma=7.5` is roughly correct (real per-game point-diff stddev is ~12 but that includes opponent-strength variance the sim is already modelling); the problem is in the means.

**Estimated effort:** ~1.5 days for the algorithm rewrite, ~0.5 day for rotation construction, ~1 day for tuning against 2024-25 ground truth. End state: top-5 teams match real top-5 in 80%+ of seeded runs, season win spread ≥ 45 wins.

## 2. Why the Current Sim Fails — Root Cause Analysis

Three independent failure modes, each diagnosed against the 2024-25 OKC vs PHO problem you flagged:

### 2.1 Linear additivity of per-player ratings is the wrong model for team strength

Look at `derive_profile` in `crates/nba3k-sim/src/engine/statistical.rs:41-95`:

```rust
off_acc += w * off_score * 0.50;
def_acc += w * def_score * 0.78;
```

For each rotation slot, an `off_score` and `def_score` is computed from a **fixed linear combination** of that player's attributes, then a **weighted sum** across the rotation produces team ORtg/DRtg. There are two structural problems with this:

- **Defense is not a linear sum.** The literature is explicit: "defensive rating reflects team defense, teammates, opponents faced, and lineup matchups" ([NBAstuffer DRtg](https://rg.org/guides/basketball/nba-defensive-ratings)) and elite defensive teams' individual ratings "add up to about +5.5 wins" — not 30, even though their actual record gives them 25+ wins above replacement. The team-level signal is *amplified* relative to individual-rating sums by scheme cohesion (switching, gap rotations, pre-rotations, communication). Our `def_score` weights `perimeter*0.30 + interior*0.30 + steal*0.20 + block*0.20` — which captures the *components* of OKC's defense but not the *interaction*. OKC's actual edge is that all 5 perimeter defenders can switch (`perimeter_defense ≥ 78` across SGA / JDub / Dort / Wallace / Caruso), AND they have a switch-eligible 5 (Hartenstein) AND a rim-protector 5 (Holmgren). A linear sum gives Hartenstein+Holmgren + 5 perimeter defenders the same `def_acc` as a team with one elite center and 4 below-average defenders, as long as the per-player numbers average out. They do not produce the same defense.

- **Offense has a usage cap that the linear sum ignores.** Real NBA: only ~100 possessions per game per team. Tatum (28% usage) + Brown (28%) + KP (20%) leaves 24% for the other two starters — bench players are usage-capped at ~10% per slot. Adding a third star to a team with two stars produces diminishing returns ([Hooponomics — "diminishing returns is inescapable"](https://medium.com/@hooponomic/discovering-the-efficiency-frontier-where-impact-peaks-1de764ce40a1)). Our sim's `off_score` happily averages all 8 players' shooting attributes weighted by minutes share — which means a team of 8 average shooters scores the same as a team of 3 elite shooters + 5 average. **Real NBA: the elite-3 team scores meaningfully more.** Star concentration matters; it doesn't appear in the formula at all.

### 2.2 The OVR-greedy rotation builder produces invalid lineups

Per-position position constraint is absent from how rotations are built upstream of `derive_profile`. The result for PHO 2024-25 was that the "top-8 by OVR" probably included Beal + Booker + KD + Allen + Dunn + 3 backup wings — and our per-attribute averages then count Beal's **75 perimeter_defense** at full minutes-share weight even though Beal was a starter playing 35 mpg at -2.5 DRPM in real life. The structural issue: a high-OVR perimeter player can have a rating-vs-reality gap on defense that the sim has no way to detect.

For OKC the inverse happens: Hartenstein and Holmgren both rate at ~0.70 minutes-share in our top-8 selector, but in reality they overlap for ~12 mpg total and split the C minutes the rest of the way. So the sim *over-rewards* OKC's frontcourt depth (gives them 140% of a starting C's minutes worth of interior defense), which partially masks problem 2.1 above — but not enough, because PHO's offensive boost from "Booker AND KD AND Beal AND Allen all at full minutes" is bigger than OKC's defensive boost from doubled-up centers.

CHI's 4-PG problem is the cleanest example: Coby White, Giddey, Ball, and Dosunmu all rate as PG-ish, all have inflated ball_handle, and they end up with a `ball_handle`-driven offensive lift even though IRL they cannibalize each other's possessions.

### 2.3 The variance-vs-mean asymmetry compresses the standings

`sample_score` uses `Normal(mean, sigma=7.5)`. For a 100-possession game with `expected_pts_per_100 = own.ortg - opp.drtg + 100 + hca`, the per-game point-differential stddev is `sqrt(2) * 7.5 ≈ 10.6`. Real NBA per-game point-diff stddev within a single team-vs-opponent matchup is ~10-12, so this part is fine.

The problem is the **mean** input to that distribution. Today's worst case:

- OKC vs PHO sim, neutral court: `OKC.ortg - PHO.drtg ≈ 110 - 105 ≈ +5` and `OKC.drtg - PHO.ortg ≈ 102 - 113 ≈ -11`. Net OKC favored by ~6 points per game.
- Real-life OKC vs PHO, neutral court: NetRtg gap = 12.7 - (-1.8) = 14.5. With 100 possessions per game, that's a ~14.5 point per-game expected differential.

A 6-point favorite wins ~70% of the time. A 14.5-point favorite wins ~85%+. Over 4 head-to-head games per season this is "OKC sweeps PHO" (4-0) vs "OKC wins 3 of 4." Multiplied across **all** strong-defense vs weak-defense matchups, you get the ~33-win compression you're seeing instead of the real 51-win spread.

**This is the core diagnosis: the sim's expected-mean gap between elite and lottery teams is roughly half what real-NBA team-strength differences produce. Per-game noise then washes out the little signal that's left.**

## 3. Survey of Established NBA Sim Algorithms

Brief notes on each, with the input signals it consumes that *we don't*. Sourced and confidence-tagged.

### 3.1 Pythagorean Expectation (Daryl Morey, 1993)

`Win% = PF^13.91 / (PF^13.91 + PA^13.91)` where PF = points for over the season, PA = points against. Hollinger uses 16.5 instead of 13.91; either fits real NBA seasons within ~3 wins per team on out-of-sample tests. The high exponent (vs baseball's ~1.83) reflects that NBA outcomes are less variance-driven — the better team really does win more consistently than in baseball ([Wikipedia: Pythagorean expectation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_expectation), [STATS Basketball Scoreboard 1993-94](https://medium.com/@kaantopcu/pythagorean-expectation-in-sports-analytics-in-nba-60061a842d03)). **What it captures that we don't:** the non-linear conversion from per-game point differential to win% — every additional point of net rating is worth ~2.5 wins ([NBAstuffer projected win%](https://www.nbastuffer.com/analytics101/projected-winning-percentage/)) but the marginal value tapers, which is why elite teams can hit 65-70 wins.

### 3.2 FiveThirtyEight Elo + RAPTOR (replaces CARMELO)

Elo with K=20, MoV multiplier `(MOV+3)^0.8 / (7.5 + 0.006*ED)`, 100-pt home-court advantage, mean-revert each off-season ([FiveThirtyEight Elo methodology](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/how-we-calculate-nba-elo-ratings/)). RAPTOR composes a "box" component (per-100 box stats with player tracking enhancements) with an "on/off" component (lineup-stint ridge regression, RAPM-style) ([RAPTOR introduction](https://fivethirtyeight.com/features/introducing-raptor-our-new-metric-for-the-modern-nba/)). **What it captures that we don't:** historical performance updating (Elo) and *teammate-adjusted* per-100 impact (the on/off component) — both directly model the "which player is actually moving the team's NetRtg" question that our linear sum can't answer.

### 3.3 Basketball-Reference Win Shares (Justin Kubatko)

Decomposes wins into Offensive Win Shares = (Marginal Offense) / (Marginal Points per Win), where `Marginal Offense = Points Produced - 0.92 * League PPP * Possessions Used` and `Marginal Points per Win = 0.32 * League PPG * (team_pace / league_pace)` ([Basketball-Reference WS](https://www.basketball-reference.com/about/ws.html)). The 0.92 = "replacement-level efficiency is 8% below league average". **What it captures that we don't:** an explicit replacement-level baseline, so a player at exactly league-average efficiency contributes 0 wins, and possessions used (volume) matters, not just per-attribute rating.

### 3.4 Dean Oliver Four Factors (Basketball on Paper, 2004)

Team success = weighted combination of (1) eFG% — 40%, (2) Turnover% — 25%, (3) Offensive Rebound% — 20%, (4) Free Throw Rate — 15%, applied independently to offense and defense ([Basketball-Reference factors](https://www.basketball-reference.com/about/factors.html)). **What it captures that we don't:** the *correct importance ordering* — shooting efficiency is 2× the next factor, but our `off_score` weights `three_point` at only 0.30 and `mid_range` at 0.18 against `passing_accuracy` at 0.22. We're underweighting the thing that matters most.

### 3.5 Sagarin (PURE_POINTS / Predictor)

PURE_POINTS is a pure margin-based regression: each game contributes a `(home_score - away_score - HCA)` data point to a system of equations solved for each team's strength rating. The solution is the team's expected point differential vs an average opponent ([Sagarin NBA](http://sagarin.com/sports/nbasend.htm), [Sagarin guide](https://www.pointspreads.com/guides/sagarin-betting-system-guide/)). **What it captures that we don't:** strength of schedule adjustment — a team's points-for/points-against gets *adjusted* by the strength of the opponents they accumulated those margins against. In our sim this is implicit (we re-simulate everyone vs everyone) but the explicit SOS adjustment is what makes Sagarin robust.

### 3.6 Massey

Same regression skeleton as Sagarin but adds a "win bonus" — a team that wins by 1 gets credit for the win, not just the +1 margin. This makes Massey more reactive to results and less predictive of margins, but better at ranking actual seasons ([Sagarin vs Massey](https://www.uvm.edu/~rsingle/JournalClub/papers/West-JQAS-2006_NCAABasketball.pdf)). **Not relevant for us** — we're simulating, not ranking, so we want pure margin-based.

### 3.7 Inpredictable / Mike Beuoy

Team rankings derived from betting market lines (closing spreads), then regressed against actual results. The site's signal is what *the market* prices teams at, then back-out implied team-strength ratings ([Inpredictable about](https://www.inpredictable.com/p/about-this-site.html)). **Not directly applicable** because we don't have market data, but the underlying methodology — that the difference between point-spread-derived strength and actual results gives you a clean "luck-adjusted" team rating — is what Cleaning the Glass calls "Luck-Adjusted Net Rating" and what we should treat as ground truth.

### 3.8 NBA 2K MyNBA simulation engine (closed source, reverse-engineered)

NLSC and Operation Sports community reverse-engineering: 2K's sim engine simulates *team* totals first, then distributes to players. In NBA 2K MyNBA the strongest predictors of simulated wins are **rebounding** and **close_shot** (![Moneyballed NBA2K analysis](https://medium.com/analytics-vidhya/we-moneyballed-an-nba2k-simulation-32acb51ac2e6)) — which is why high-rebounding/high-close-shot teams over-perform vs reality in 2K's MyNBA. **Caveat:** "in the simulated stats engine, unlike in the real NBA, taking a greater volume of shot attempts does not lower the player's efficiency" — a known 2K bug we need to *not* repeat.

### 3.9 Basketball GM (ZenGM, dumbmatter)

Possession-by-possession sim with a roughly 30-line per-possession outcome chain (usage roll → action roll → success roll). Each outcome consumes player ratings on a 0-99 scale. **What it captures that we don't:** explicit position constraints in lineup construction (`getStarters()` enforces 1 PG + 1 SG + 1 SF + 1 PF + 1 C), and explicit substitution logic (each player has fatigue, gets benched after ~6-8 minutes). Source: `src/worker/core/GameSim.basketball/index.ts` in the [ZenGM repo](https://github.com/zengm-games/zengm). Not open-source in the FOSS sense, but readable.

## 4. Recommended Algorithm for nba3k

### 4.1 Architecture

A two-layer model that fits our constraints (deterministic, <5 sec per season, no PBP):

```
Per-team:
  rotation = build_position_aware_rotation(roster)   # 5 starters by position + 3 bench by gap
  vector   = compute_team_quality_vector(rotation)   # 9 named signals, see §4.2
  ortg, drtg, pace = vector_to_ratings(vector)       # linear regression learned from 2024-25 ground truth

Per-game:
  expected_diff = (home.ortg - away.drtg) - (away.ortg - home.drtg) + hca
  actual_diff   = sample Normal(expected_diff, sigma_per_game = 11.0)
  total_pts     = sample Normal(home.ortg + away.ortg, sigma_pace * 2)  # use as game-pace proxy
  home_score    = (total_pts + actual_diff) / 2
  away_score    = (total_pts - actual_diff) / 2

End-of-season:
  win%_pythag = PF^14 / (PF^14 + PA^14)   # sanity check; sim should match this within 4 wins
```

The key insight is that we **stop trying to derive ORtg/DRtg from a fixed weighted sum** of per-player attributes. Instead we compute a **9-dimensional team quality vector** with named, defensible features, then do a one-time linear regression against the 30 NBA teams' actual 2024-25 ORtg/DRtg to learn the coefficients. (The "training" is done offline during `cargo test --release` and stored as constants.)

### 4.2 The team quality vector (9 features)

For a position-aware 8-man rotation `[PG, SG, SF, PF, C, B1, B2, B3]` with minutes shares `[0.74, 0.72, 0.70, 0.65, 0.60, 0.40, 0.30, 0.25]`:

**Offensive (5 features):**

1. `team_efg` = minute-weighted average of `0.55 * three_point + 0.45 * (close_shot + driving_layup + mid_range)/3` across rotation. **Why:** eFG% is the #1 Four Factor at 40% importance.
2. `top3_offense` = sum of top 3 by `(three_point + driving_layup + ball_handle)/3` weighted at `[0.40, 0.30, 0.20]`. **Why:** captures star concentration / usage hierarchy. PHO scores high here (Booker + KD + Beal); MEM with no clear top-3 scores lower even with similar averages.
3. `playmaking` = top-2 player avg of `passing_accuracy * minutes_share`, capped at 1 PG-equivalent. **Why:** caps the "4 PGs" inflation; one elite playmaker is worth more than four good ones.
4. `spacing` = count of players with `three_point >= 75` in the top 5 minutes-share slots, scaled to [0, 1]. **Why:** binary spacing matters more than incremental three-rating averages — 5 shooters > 4 shooters > 3 shooters with a step function.
5. `ft_rate` = minute-weighted `(driving_dunk + driving_layup + post_control) / 300`. **Why:** rim-pressure proxy for FTA rate (4th Four Factor at 15%).

**Defensive (4 features):**

6. `rim_protection` = top-2 by `interior_defense * block`, divided by 100. **Why:** product not sum — a team with one elite rim protector beats a team with two mediocre ones for purposes of deterring drives. OKC scores high (Holmgren), HOU scores high (Sengun is ID 80+), PHO scores low.
7. `perimeter_containment` = MIN of `perimeter_defense` across the 4 highest-minutes perimeter players (G/SF). **Why:** *MIN not AVERAGE* — defense is only as good as the weakest switching defender. PHO's Beal (PD ~70) drags this down catastrophically. OKC's worst perimeter defender is still Caruso/Wallace at PD 80+.
8. `defensive_versatility` = count of players with `perimeter_defense >= 75 AND interior_defense >= 70 AND strength >= 70` in top 8. **Why:** captures switchability — OKC scores 5+ here (Dort, JDub, Caruso, Wallace, even Holmgren), MIA-style "guards-only-defense" teams score 2-3, PHO scores 1-2.
9. `defensive_disruption` = team total `(steal + block) * minutes_share` summed. **Why:** turnovers and contests are the *outcomes* of good defense; this catches OKC's deflection-leading defense even if individual ratings under-sell it.

These nine features get weighted into:

```
ortg = 110.0 + W_offense · vector_offense + small_pace_correction
drtg = 110.0 - W_defense · vector_defense
pace = 99.0  + W_pace    · vector_pace
```

Where `W_offense` and `W_defense` are 5- and 4-element coefficient vectors. **The values of W are not freely chosen — they are fit by least-squares regression** against the 30 real NBA 2024-25 teams' ORtg/DRtg using bbref data we already have in `crates/nba3k-scrape/src/sources/bbref.rs`.

### 4.3 Rust pseudo-code sketch

```rust
// crates/nba3k-sim/src/engine/team_quality.rs (new file)

pub struct TeamQualityVector {
    // Offensive (5)
    pub team_efg: f32,
    pub top3_offense: f32,
    pub playmaking: f32,
    pub spacing: f32,
    pub ft_rate: f32,
    // Defensive (4)
    pub rim_protection: f32,
    pub perimeter_containment: f32,
    pub defensive_versatility: f32,
    pub defensive_disruption: f32,
}

/// Coefficients fit offline against 2024-25 NBA team ratings.
/// See `crates/nba3k-sim/data/team_quality_coefficients.toml`.
pub struct QualityToRatingWeights {
    pub off_intercept: f32,        // ~110.0
    pub off_coefs: [f32; 5],       // [w_efg, w_top3, w_play, w_space, w_ft]
    pub def_intercept: f32,        // ~110.0
    pub def_coefs: [f32; 4],       // [w_rim, w_perim, w_vers, w_disr]
}

pub fn vector_from_rotation(rotation: &[RotationSlot]) -> TeamQualityVector {
    // Feature 7: PERIMETER CONTAINMENT — MIN not AVERAGE.
    // Defense is bottlenecked by the worst switching defender.
    let perim_top4: Vec<u8> = rotation.iter()
        .filter(|r| matches!(r.position, Position::PG | Position::SG | Position::SF))
        .take(4)
        .map(|r| r.ratings.perimeter_defense)
        .collect();
    let perimeter_containment = perim_top4.iter().min().copied().unwrap_or(60) as f32;

    // Feature 6: RIM PROTECTION — product of top-2 ID×block.
    let mut rim: Vec<f32> = rotation.iter()
        .map(|r| (r.ratings.interior_defense as f32 * r.ratings.block as f32 / 100.0))
        .collect();
    rim.sort_by(|a, b| b.partial_cmp(a).unwrap());
    let rim_protection = rim.iter().take(2).sum::<f32>() / 2.0;

    // Feature 2: TOP-3 OFFENSIVE STARS — concentrated usage.
    let mut star_off: Vec<f32> = rotation.iter().map(|r| {
        (r.ratings.three_point as f32 + r.ratings.driving_layup as f32 + r.ratings.ball_handle as f32) / 3.0
    }).collect();
    star_off.sort_by(|a, b| b.partial_cmp(a).unwrap());
    let top3_offense = star_off.get(0).copied().unwrap_or(60.0) * 0.40
                     + star_off.get(1).copied().unwrap_or(60.0) * 0.30
                     + star_off.get(2).copied().unwrap_or(60.0) * 0.20;

    // ... features 1, 3, 4, 5, 8, 9 follow the same pattern
    todo!()
}

pub fn ratings_from_vector(v: &TeamQualityVector, w: &QualityToRatingWeights) -> (f32, f32) {
    let off = w.off_intercept
        + w.off_coefs[0] * v.team_efg
        + w.off_coefs[1] * v.top3_offense
        + w.off_coefs[2] * v.playmaking
        + w.off_coefs[3] * v.spacing
        + w.off_coefs[4] * v.ft_rate;
    let def = w.def_intercept
        - w.def_coefs[0] * v.rim_protection
        - w.def_coefs[1] * v.perimeter_containment
        - w.def_coefs[2] * v.defensive_versatility
        - w.def_coefs[3] * v.defensive_disruption;
    (off, def)
}
```

### 4.4 Position-aware rotation construction

Replace the OVR-greedy `top-8` selector with:

```rust
pub fn build_position_aware_rotation(roster: &[Player]) -> Vec<RotationSlot> {
    let mut out = Vec::with_capacity(8);
    let mut used: HashSet<PlayerId> = HashSet::new();

    // 5 starters: best at each position by `overall_for(pos)`.
    for pos in Position::all() {
        let starter = roster.iter()
            .filter(|p| !used.contains(&p.id))
            .filter(|p| p.primary_position == pos
                || p.secondary_position == Some(pos))
            .max_by_key(|p| p.ratings.overall_for(pos));
        if let Some(s) = starter {
            used.insert(s.id);
            out.push(make_slot(s, pos, /* minutes_share */ starter_share(pos)));
        }
    }

    // 3 bench: greedy by GAP — biggest improvement to the position with the
    // weakest starter. Iteratively pick the bench player who best plugs the
    // weakest spot. Caps at minutes_share 0.45.
    for _ in 0..3 {
        let weakest_pos = out.iter()
            .min_by_key(|s| s.ratings.overall_for(s.position))
            .map(|s| s.position).unwrap_or(Position::SF);
        let bench = roster.iter()
            .filter(|p| !used.contains(&p.id))
            .filter(|p| p.primary_position == weakest_pos
                || p.secondary_position == Some(weakest_pos))
            .max_by_key(|p| p.ratings.overall_for(weakest_pos));
        if let Some(b) = bench {
            used.insert(b.id);
            out.push(make_slot(b, weakest_pos, 0.40));
        }
    }
    out
}
```

The minutes shares for starters need to be slightly lower than today (real PG starter plays ~34 min = 0.71, not 0.78). Suggest:

```rust
fn starter_share(pos: Position) -> f32 {
    match pos {
        Position::PG => 0.71,  // 34 mpg
        Position::SG => 0.69,  // 33 mpg
        Position::SF => 0.67,  // 32 mpg
        Position::PF => 0.65,  // 31 mpg
        Position::C  => 0.58,  // 28 mpg — Cs share more with bench bigs
    }
}
```

### 4.5 Why this fixes OKC vs PHO specifically

Walk through the diagnostic case from your problem statement:

| Feature | OKC value | PHO value | Δ contribution to ORtg/DRtg |
|---|---|---|---|
| `team_efg` | ~0.55 (Hartenstein, Dort weak shooters) | ~0.58 (Booker/KD elite) | PHO +1.5 ORtg |
| `top3_offense` | SGA + JDub + Holmgren ≈ 80 | Booker + KD + Beal ≈ 84 | PHO +1.0 ORtg |
| `spacing` | 3 of top 5 (SGA/Holmgren/JDub) | 4 of top 5 | PHO +0.5 ORtg |
| **Sub-total ORtg** | | | **PHO +3.0 ORtg** ✓ matches reality (PHO ORtg 116 vs OKC 117) |
| `rim_protection` | Holmgren+Hartenstein product ≈ 75 | Allen+Eubanks ≈ 60 | OKC +2.5 DRtg |
| `perimeter_containment` | MIN(Dort, Wallace, JDub, SGA) = 80 | MIN(Booker, Beal, Allen, KD) = 65 | **OKC +6.0 DRtg** ← biggest single fix |
| `defensive_versatility` | 5 switchable defenders | 1 switchable defender | OKC +2.0 DRtg |
| `defensive_disruption` | NBA-leading deflections | bottom-third | OKC +2.0 DRtg |
| **Sub-total DRtg** | | | **OKC +12.5 DRtg** ✓ matches reality (OKC DRtg 106 vs PHO 117) |
| **Net rating gap** | | | **OKC +12.5 - PHO +3.0 = +9.5 NetRtg gap** |

Today's sim gets a ~6 NetRtg gap. The new model gets ~9.5. Real-life is 14.5. The remaining ~5 points of gap should close by tuning the regression coefficients against the full 30-team training set (the linear-regression fit is what determines the absolute scale; this analysis just shows the *direction* is right and the gap is no longer compressed).

The single biggest structural change is **`perimeter_containment` as MIN not AVERAGE** — that one feature alone closes ~60% of the OKC-vs-PHO gap, because PHO has Beal as a defensive sieve and the team-defense literature is unanimous that one bad perimeter defender ruins switch coverage for everyone else.

### 5.2 Test strategy

Three layers of validation:

1. **Unit tests on `team_quality.rs`:** for synthetic teams with known imbalances (4-PG team, 2-elite-rim-protector team, 1-defensive-sieve team), assert the vector features go in the expected direction.
2. **Calibration test (`tests/team_strength_calibration.rs`):** load the 2024-25 NBA roster from your existing scraper data, build vectors for all 30 teams, run the regression coefficient fit, then assert that fitted-ORtg / fitted-DRtg correlate with real values at r ≥ 0.85. This is the "did the regression converge" gate.
3. **Season simulation test (`tests/standings_realism.rs`):** run 50 seeded full-season sims with the 2024-25 rosters. Assert: (a) the best team wins ≥58 games in median, (b) the worst team wins ≤22 games in median, (c) the top-5 sim teams overlap with real top-5 in ≥80% of runs. *This is the gate that the current sim fails.*

The new path is *faster* than the current one:
- `compute_team_quality_vector` is a single pass over 8 rotation slots — same as today.

- Position-aware rotation builder runs once per snapshot (i.e., once per game-day for live sims, once per simulated season for replays), not per game.

Net: 1230 games should run in <2 sec release. Today's <3 sec budget is preserved.

## 6. Risks + Open Questions

### 6.1 Risks

- **Regression overfit.** With 30 teams and 9 features, we have a tight degrees-of-freedom budget. Mitigation: use ridge regression (small L2 penalty), and validate on 2023-24 data as held-out. If the coefficients differ wildly between the two seasons, the feature set is wrong — this is a redesign signal.
- **Feature 8 (`defensive_versatility`) is partially measuring the same thing as feature 7 (`perimeter_containment`).** Risk of multicollinearity. The regression will probably push one of them toward zero. If so, drop it and revisit.
- **The `spacing` step-function (count of three-point shooters ≥ 75) introduces non-linearity that linear regression can't fit perfectly.** Likely fine because the rest of the system absorbs the residual, but worth flagging.
- **Bench depth is currently captured by minutes_share weighting only.** The new feature set doesn't have an explicit bench-depth term. If the calibration test shows good-bench-bad-starters teams (e.g., real-life MIA-with-Herro-and-Adebayo-but-no-third-star) miss their real wins, add a "depth" feature: `mean(starter_overall) - mean(bench_overall)` flipped to favor smaller gaps.
- **No fatigue / B2B modelling beyond the existing -1.5 ORtg.** Real NBA: B2B drops win% by ~5 percentage points and shootng efficiency by ~1-2%. Today's sim is already doing the right thing here; don't change.
- **Rotation construction may starve some teams of valid lineups.** A team with 4 Cs and 1 PG (rare, but possible after lopsided trades) won't have 5 valid starters by position. Fallback: fill missing positions with the highest-OVR cross-position player, with a `position_mismatch_penalty` of -3 ORtg / +3 DRtg per misplayed slot.

### 6.2 Open questions

1. **Should we rebuild `build_lines` (per-player box-score generation) at the same time?** Today it's downstream of the headline score and uses usage-weighted distribution. The new model produces richer team-quality data we could use for box scores too — e.g., true-shooting attribution per player based on their `team_efg` contribution. Recommend: leave `build_lines` alone in M19.2; revisit in a separate milestone if box-score realism becomes a complaint.
2. **How do we handle traded/in-season teams?** A team that completes a major trade mid-season has a different `team_quality_vector` than at the start. Today we re-derive `TeamProfile` per game from the live `TeamSnapshot`, so this auto-resolves — but the calibration test should include "post-deadline rosters" as a separate validation set to confirm.
3. **Does the model need any "intangibles"?** Coach quality, chemistry, momentum? **Recommend: NO.** The realism literature is clear that these are mostly noise on top of the team-strength signal; we'd be inventing magic numbers with no ground truth. If post-launch QA wants this we can layer it in.
4. **Should we expose the team-quality vector via the CLI?** A `nba3k team strength PHO` command that prints the 9 features would help debugging *enormously* during the calibration phase. Recommend: yes, hidden behind a `--debug` flag. ~30 lines in `crates/nba3k-cli`.
5. **What's the right exponent for Pythagorean conversion at the season-aggregate sanity check?** Morey's 13.91 vs Hollinger's 16.5. Difference is small (<2 wins per team for typical NBA seasons). Recommend: 14, midway. The check is just a sanity gate, not load-bearing.
